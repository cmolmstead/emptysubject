Fragmented For You

“The subject cannot know God’s necessity with any certainty, he can only be certain of his need to know, a need that stems precisely from his [our] ‘nullity.’” Katerina Deligiorgi

Our desire, our quest, the thirst to know God, the certainty of a higher power, or at the very least a realm that is more and beyond this one, stems not from the arrogance of humanity, an immodesty to know all, nor a will to master the world but rather a desire to master ourselves. We seek God’s existence so that we may guarantee our own. The logic follows an inversion of Sartre’s famous axiom that ‘existence precedes essence;’ that is, the brute and bare fact that ‘we are’ is so before we ask, ‘why we are.’ But do we not instead feel more powerfully inclined that if there is no why, no meaning or support for this existence, that existence collapses altogether?

In Sartre also is to be found a triumph of the self, of the subject. Yet, we always go looking for ourselves outside of ourselves? We are divided. We seek what’s ‘in here’ ‘out there.’ We find ‘our self’ in the Other, whether it be God, a mother, or a lover. Just as the lyric of the song Old Bone goes:

And I could ask for what I want, so that you could tell me what you need

But this relation is marked by an impossibility. Just as we can never know God, only our desire to, we can never know the Other. For if we did, it would no longer truly be Other; at the root, there must be a minimal division and alienation. And thus we cannot (fully) be. But it is out of impossibility that comes possibility. For what is love, what is fidelity, other than the will to surmount this impossibility? The unwavering desire to know, to seek the Other is what defines us.

We are not, and it is ‘this not’ we are.

To love, in spite of its own impossibility, is to love truly.

“Desire is always the desire of the Other.” Jacques Lacan
